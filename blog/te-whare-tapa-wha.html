Te Whare Tapa Whā

Creating a wellbeing plan using Tā Mason Durie’s Te Whare Tapa Whā model is a powerful way to ensure a balanced and holistic approach to health. This model breaks down wellbeing into four key dimensions, often visualized as the four walls of a house, each supporting and strengthening the others. Here's how I'm applying Te Whare Tapa Whā to my life:

Taha Tinana (Physical Wellbeing)

Taha tinana is all about physical health—how our bodies grow, feel, and move, and how we take care of them. For me, this includes:

  • Regular walks. It's easy to get absorbed in a coding session, but getting up regularly to take a walk helps me check in with myself: am I hungry, drinking enough water, or just need a bathroom break?
  • Gym 2-3 times a week. This takes discipline and setting boundaries, making sure I leave work at a good time.
  • Brushing teeth. Good oral hygiene is crucial for preventing sickness.
  • Eating well. Home-cooked meals are a staple for maintaining good nutrition.
  • Drinking water. Staying hydrated is essential. My Frank Green bottle makes this easy and attractive.

Taha Hinengaro (Emotional and Mental Wellbeing)

Taha hinengaro encompasses our mind, heart, conscience, thoughts, and feelings. It's about how we feel and how we communicate and think. My plan includes:

  • Time away from the computer. Setting boundaries and taking breaks to avoid burnout.
  • Quiet and relaxation time. Watching YouTube videos, movies, reading, and just relaxing on the couch—essential for an introvert like me to recharge.
  • Recognising burnout early. Taking extended breaks early on can improve long-term productivity.

Taha Wairua (Spiritual Wellbeing)

Wairua can mean different things to different people—faith, religious beliefs, or a connection to the universe or something sacred. For me:

  • Quiet time. Spending time doing very little and being aware of myself helps keep me grounded.
  • Walking commute along the waterfront. This daily connection with nature is vital for my sense of peace and spirituality.

Taha Whānau (Family Wellbeing)

Taha whānau focuses on our sense of belonging, who we care about, and who we share our lives with. My plan includes:

  • Monthly date night with Michelle, my wife. Making a regular effort to stay connected as we are each other's greatest support.
  • Walking to work with Michelle. This is a great time to wind up, wind down, and debrief about the day.
  • Lunchtime dates with Michelle. Semi-regular lunch catch-ups to support each other, especially as she runs her business by herself.
  • Social time with classmates/workmates. Building relationships beyond work goals to create stronger connections.
  • Hangout nights and board games with neighbors/friends. Taking time for fun helps maintain connections and also supports mental and emotional wellbeing.

This holistic approach helps ensure that no part of my wellbeing is neglected. Each dimension supports the others, creating a robust structure for a balanced and healthy life.
