Thinking like a programmer

We are thinking about problem-solving in the context of programming. It's been interesting to reflect on my process for tackling challenges. For basic queries or when I need a bit of extra insight, I've found AI tools like chatgpt surprisingly useful. It's like having a rubber duck that talks back, someone to bounce off my thoughts, and get nudged in the right direction. That said, I'm still the type to be skeptical of just being handed an answer. I want to understand the how and why before I even get to the what.

It sounds simple, but it's often these little things that help me see problems from a different angle.

Looking online, there's a lot of discussion about how to think like a programmer, and a recurring theme is the importance of breaking down problems into smaller, more manageable parts. I've personally also valued building models or simpler versions of a problem to help wrap my head around it. It's almost like creating a mini playground where you can experiment and learn without the pressure of the bigger, more complex problem looming over you.

I also learned about flipped learning, which seems to be the approach we're taking in this course. Instead of using face-to-face time for lectures, it's used for more interactive, hands-on learning, while the information dump is done independently. It's a bit of an adjustment for me, especially because I prefer to clarify things as I'm learning. But I can see how this approach can encourage more active engagement, and I'm excited to see how it will influence my learning journey.
